North Korea, Iran, Germany? Is this really the new “Axis” of Evil? Apparently so according to reports that German PM Angela Merkel is seeking to “get hold of [stolen bank account] data” of German citizens who have accounts in Swiss banks. It seems that the Germans are in the market for the new commodity of choice for organized crime, personal and financial data.

The Germans have been offered 1,300 names of German citizens for 2.5 million Euro ($3.48 million). That is $2,676 per person. I think they are over paying. Thirty pieces of silver cost less than $500 at the current price per ounce.

The fact that a government is engaged in shady dealings doesn't really come as a surprise.  I think most of us already know that governments usually do bad things behind closed doors.  Even the bravado of doing something so obviously shady in public is no longer shocking.  What concerns me is the lack of respect for a basic human right, the right to have confidential relationships and transactions without the oversight of big brother, ahem, excuse me Ms. Merkel, big sister.  Had there been sufficient suspicion of a crime prior to requesting the records, the Swiss government would have been able to provide the names of the account holders and any account information related to the crime.  I would have less of a problem with that kind of disclosure.  But a blanket disclosure of data, with no evidence, and no suspicion, is dangerous.

Part of the problem is the way we conceptualize personal data.  In order for personal data to be protected from such attacks, it should be treated by the law the same way it is treated in commerce, as a form of personal property. Given its value to governments and criminal gangs, why is it not property? Bills like Senate Bill 1490, imposing fines and criminal penalties for institutions who house data but don't protect it, and H.R. 2630, giving doctors and patients the right to opt out of information sharing, begin to surround personal information with protection as if it were property.

Fractional Reserve Banks like Goldman Sachs and JP Morgan Chase want to treat the data that you deposit into their transactional databases as if it were currency to be traded like an asset on its balance sheet. An asset they can loan out to others for profit, much like the money you deposit in their coffers. The problem is, there is a difference between the data you deposit, and the funds that you deposit into a bank, the data is worth a lot more. So far they are benefitting tremendously from the information arbitrage because they can force you, by law, to produce all kinds of information and then share it until you tell them to stop.  They earn money from the unwitting consumer.

The big bad banks aren't the only ones to blame. They are prohibited from entering into a confidential relationship with their customers and clients for banking services. The Bank Secrecy Act, FinCEN, and other “know your customer” rules impose information disclosures on bank customers and severe restrictions on economic activity. Suspicious Activity Reports and Currency Transaction Reports require even mundane transactions to be reported to government bank surveillance officials. Like a system that requires the reporting of suspicious library books being checked out or subversive websites being visited, you are probably already in their transactional databases.

Why is bank privacy so important? Privacy in our every day transactions is necessary to preserve fundamental human rights. A client facing potential legal trouble may see a lawyer and be completely protected in the communication, even if the client is confessing mass homicide of a school bus full of international aid workers. The attorney can't, and of course does not, do anything to further the crime and does the ethical thing by counseling the client to do what is both moral and legal going forward. He still cannot violate his obligation to keep his client's secrets confidential and reveal that his client has committed these crimes. If the attorney does so, they may be subject to serious penalties. There is similar respect for priests or doctors, but not of banks and banking information. Are the banks so morally bankrupt that they are incapable of acting with the same integrity of a priest, doctor or lawyer?  Although these confidentiality rules have some negative effects, they allow potential whistleblowers to see a whistleblower attorney without fear of unwanted disclosure, it allows a victim of abuse to seek help without placing their life a trisk, and a host of other benefits to society.

So, if we view personal data as personal property, what is Merkie doing that is so wrong? Isn't she fighting crime and stuff like that? Most states outlaw knowingly dealing in stolen property. California law is written so that a person who 1)receives stolen property and 2) knows it to be stolen, goes to jail. This is typical of most states. So, would she be guilty if she went through with it?

Lets assume Merkie does come into possession of the stolen ciphers, she will easily satisfy the first requirement, possession.  The Swiss Finance Minister has indicated that the information is stolen data, and that his own government will not cooperate in furthering the crime. So either Merkie has such a poor research staff that she doesn't know that the information is stolen, or she is not smart enough to understand the concept of stealing. I think it is safe to say that she knows it is stolen. So it looks like, if she were to complete this transaction within the state of California, she would, hopefully, be headed off to jail, without passing Go and without collecting $200!

Confidential relationships and transactions are a fundamental right.  Only upon some significant degree of suspicion of a serious crime based on specific facts should any kind of confidentiality be required to be disclosed.  Protecting personal privacy is vital to avoiding serious harm in a modern world. Governments and organized crime are willing to violate the law to compromise personal information to the detriment of all.  Everyone can begin by affirmatively opting out of any information sharing plan that their bank or lender has.  Learning How To Vanish, engaging in lawful hawala banking transactions and opting out of information sharing is a good start to protecting your personal and economic interests.
